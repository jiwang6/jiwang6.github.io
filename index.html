Reading between the Lines: How Historical Cryptographies Reflect Their Contemporary Education

Jim
Sep 22, 2020 · 11 min read

If you’ve recently competed in any variety of cyber competitions, you’ve undoubtedly encountered a cryptography problem and had to convert ciphertext into some plaintext. While modern problems range from menial plug-and-chug tests of understanding to advanced algorithmic analysis, their ancestors almost all involved calculations that can be completed by hand. These ciphers, such as the Vigenère cipher and even simple substitution ciphers, once guarded military, governmental, or personal secrets against unwanted prying eyes.

One question ultimately arises: How did such simple ciphers maintain any sort of secrecy in the past? The answer is simple: education. In this article, I will argue that as intensive mathematical education increased in prevalence throughout history, the role of cryptography began in strictly militaristic use, shifted into the social reals, and redeveloped into military use.

As educational standards evolved throughout history, cryptography’s usage shifted from military usage against a deemphasized mathematics curriculum, into the public sphere as mathematics education was greater realized, and back into military use as cryptographic techniques improved.

We will divide our timeline into three distinct chapters: Discovery, Understanding, and Adaptation. These chapters are marked by advanced cryptographic use against a poor mathematical education, lowered cryptographic use against an advanced mathematical education, and a resurgence of advanced cryptographic use against an advanced mathematical education respectively. Chapter 1: Discovery (500 - 400 BCE)

Cryptography began around 500 BCE as a mathematical science used primarily in the military and was designed to hide vital communications should they fall into enemy hands. The earliest example of a technique used to hide military secrets is the Greek wax tablet as shown below:


Scribes used these tablets to create records by carving letters into the wax. However, the Greek military often carved military intelligence into the wood and hid the secrets with smooth wax on top. This was used in the conflict between Greece and Persia. As mathematics writer Simon Singh details in his book, The Code Book, upon receiving the tablet, “they scraped the wax off … [and] found something written on the wood underneath. This was done; the message was revealed and read, and afterward passed on to the other Greeks” (Singh 4). This technique was used to inform the Greek military of the Persian military’s buildup and allowed the Greeks to repel Persian forces.

Technically, this method of hiding instead of encoding the information is called steganography. A more typical example of cryptography is the Spartan scytale shown below:


This tool is an example of a transposition cipher, meaning that the letters are simply rearranged. In the case of the Spartan scytale, a warfighter would wrap a belt with carved letters around a scytale and the letters would be arranged to read an important message.

Another example of ancient cryptography is the classic Caesar cipher. This cipher is an example of a monoalphabetic substitution cipher. This means that every letter in the plaintext will be substituted with a single letter that remains the same for all instances of that letter in the plain text. Below is a good visual representation of the Caesar cipher:


To decipher this cipher, one would need to know how many places shifted down each letter is. In the example above, the ciphertext is all shifted up two spaces (D becomes A, E becomes B). To decipher this code, one would only need to shift the ciphertext down two spaces.

All these ciphers seem simple in retrospect. So why were the Greeks and Romans so confident in their encryption? The answer is that the majority of their education focused on how to live the life most worth living. According to J. J. O’Connor from the University of St. Andrews, the brunt of the Greek education focused most on reaching “graceful perfection both physically and mentally” (O’Connor). Additionally, he states that what mathematical education available to the Greeks was reserved for the most upper class. Finally, he emphasizes that the Roman education further reduced the importance of mathematics. Therefore, under this educational system, both Greek and Roman militaries had no true code breakers who could decipher their codes. All secrets during this time relied on basic steganography and transposition and substitution ciphers because the education available deemphasized mathematics.

Chapter 2: Understanding (800 - 1500 CE)

As we continue past the Greeks and Romans, we see that cryptography and cryptographic studies remained relatively stagnant until 1500 CE. However, Middle Eastern mathematician Al-Kindi ended this extensive period of stagnation with pioneering code-breaking techniques such as frequency analysis.

Now, this is huge! The frequency analysis technique effectively allowed advanced codebreakers to decipher any substitution cipher. This ultimately rendered all monoalphabetic substitution ciphers, including the Caesar cipher obsolete.

In a nutshell, this method worked by analyzing the rates each character in a certain language appears. For example, in English, the letter ‘A’ appears around 8% on average. If a ciphertext has a character, the letter ‘Q’ for example, that appears around 8%, we can confidently say that the letter ‘Q’ in the ciphertext translates to the letter ‘A’ in plaintext. A commonly accepted distribution for average occurrences of letters in the English language appears below:


These techniques most likely made their way to Europe via the Silk Road. Scholar Leigh Chipman, in her journal Texts in Transit in the Medieval Mediterranean, writes that an “Important issue for the history of cryptography is the fact that Mosul was a station on the Silk Road, as shown by Marco Polo’s route to China. Venice, Polo’s homeland, was the first to establish a cipher bureau” (Chipman 80). These techniques enjoyed a newfound home in a completely new realm: diplomacy.

During the Renaissance, Italy was primarily composed of independent city-states - chief among them was Venice. As scholar Christopher Andrew writes in his book, Secret World, “To keep a close eye on their rivals, however, the city-states in Renaissance Italy established a new system of resident ambassadors” (Andrew 12). These resident ambassadors not only stored their secrets in ciphertext but were active in the breaking of the codes that encrypted other city-states’ secrets. The more skilled as a cryptographer these ambassadors were, the higher of a rank they achieved. One example of this is Giovanni Soro. Active during the early 1500’s Soro worked as a codebreaker to decipher intercepted messages between enemy city-states. Of Soro, Andrew writes “Unlike twentieth- and twenty-first- century cryptanalysts, whose identities remained officially secret during their working lives, Soro became an international celebrity” (129). This celebrity status demonstrates the importance of encoding secrets and breaking those codes. Therefore, cryptography played a vital part outside the military as a diplomatic tool during the early 1500s.

An important caveat for this technique is that it compares averages within a small sample of text to that of an entire language. Thus, this technique works best for large samples of ciphertext and often fails with small snippets of text. The Babington Plot (1586) exemplifies another instance of the struggle between code makers and codebreakers in the social realm. During this event, Queen Mary of Scotts, a Catholic Monarch, aimed to assassinate Queen Elizabeth I, a catholic ruler who sat England’s throne. Queen Mary and her co-conspirator, Anthony Babington, wished revenge against the anti-Catholic policies and actions that ultimately lead Queen Mary through a string of prisons and to the execution of Babington’s great-grandfather. Their efforts ultimately failed when a Minister, Sir Francis Walshingham, infiltrated their conspiracy and deciphered their communications. What was Babington and Queen Mary’s mistake? They used a monoalphabetic cipher to encode messages between conspirators which Walshingham decrypted. Armed with overwhelming proof of conspiracy, Queen Elizabeth’s court tried and executed both Babington and Queen Mary as shown in the depiction below.


Simply put, the world then lacked a secure cipher. With techniques realized first by Al-Kindi, medieval codebreakers could decipher the vast majority of monoalphabetic ciphers. Despite how random or unorthodox these ciphers were, education that pushed techniques such as frequency analysis crippled them all. A cryptographic vacuum now existed that required an unbreakable code, one that could fulfill the needs of diplomats, conspirators, and warfighters alike. Thankfully, Blaise de Vigenère and his revolutionary Vigenère cipher filled this vacuum in the late 1500s.

Chapter 3: Adaptation (1500 - 1900 CE)

First envisioned by Giovan Battista Bellaso in 1553 but fully realized by Blaise de Vigenère in the late 1500s, the Vigenère cipher revolutionized cryptography. This cipher is polyalphabetic, meaning that each character in plaintext has multiple character substitutions in the ciphertext, usually specified by a codeword or phrase. A visual description is below:


Just from a cursory glance, we can see that this cipher encodes messages significantly more thoroughly than any monoalphabetic cipher. These ever-shifting character substitutions make it impervious to any sort of frequency analysis. Therefore, the classical methods of decryption used extensively in renaissance Europe no longer deciphered messages using polyalphabetic methods.

This advancement in cryptographic techniques launched cryptology back into the military sphere. We can see the Vigenère cipher used during the American Civil War, almost 300 years after the cipher’s initial conception. In 1862, the Confederacy established the Confederate Signal Corps. The corps relied intensely on cryptography since communications were easily intercepted by the union. The National Cryptologic Foundation reports that transmissions were “easily intercepted by the enemy. The trick was to encrypt the messages so that they would make sense only to the intended receiver” (Foundation). Additionally, Col William Plum wrote in his report on the military telegraph during the Civil War about a telegraph operator who was “overtaken by cipher telegrams, which he could not translate” (Plum 170). Thus, we can see a return to the reliance on ciphers as well as the efficacy in using them.

These messages used a variety of ciphers to attempt encryption and chief among them was the Vigenère cipher. Scholar Carl Guarneri, in his book Lincon’s Informer, wrote “While searching through the Confederate secretary of state Judah Benjamin’s abandoned office in Richmond on April 6 [an American journalist] retrieved a cipher sheet pasted on a cylinder. On the paper was an alphabet square (called a Vigenère cipher after its sixteenth-century French inventor), which gridded the alphabet in a table of 26 letters across and 27 down” (Guarneri 367). Messages were encrypted by these squares and cipher wheels, such as the one depicted below:


Why were these codes so effective? Why hadn’t any American mathematicians broken the code? A large part of the answer lies within the American educational system at the time; it didn’t prioritize innovation. As scholar Jessica Furr writes, math and arithmetic’s “teaching was characterized by such extremes of drill and discipline that up to one-half of every school day could be spent on arithmetic, without much learning occurring” (Furr). This education deemphasized the problem-solving attributes of mathematics and focused almost exclusively on its calculus. These qualities of the American education stagnated development in code-breaking techniques and thus lead to the continued strength of the polyalphabetic cipher until Charles Babbage cracked it in 1868.

Looking Forward

It took 300 years to decrypt the Vigenère cipher and over 1200 years to develop even the fundamentals of frequency analysis. These two efforts began the feud between code makers and codebreakers that lasts even today. Nowadays, the educational backdrop for this feud has shifted toward rapid innovation and problem-solving. In return, this feud has accelerated greatly, with both code makers and codebreakers relying on increasingly experimental technologies to trump the other. As we continue into this new chapter in cryptography, we benefit from revisiting the history of cryptography. The three chapters of cryptography explored in this article warn us against educational and innovative stagnation. Should we cease to learn and innovate, we risk overlooking hidden transmissions as the Persians did, using a depreciated technology as Queen Mary of Scotts did, or remaining locked in an informationally opaque war as the Union and Confederacy were.
